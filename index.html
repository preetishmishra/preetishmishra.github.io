I am a Research Fellow at the Korea Institute for Advanced Study (KIAS), Seoul, Korea.

My research lies at the intersection of observational astronomy and galaxy formation theory. I am primarily interested in understanding how galaxies acquire and evolve their fundamental physical properties — mass, structural morphology, and internal kinematics — and how these are shaped by the dark matter halos they inhabit and the large-scale environments around them. I use data from large photometric surveys (Subaru-HSC SSP, Rubin-LSST) and spectroscopic surveys (SDSS, MaNGA, GAMA) to trace these connections. I complement this with cosmological simulations to study the origin of these scaling relations and understand the physical processes that give rise to them.

Research Interests

Galaxy Sizes and Dark Matter Halos

Using Subaru Hyper Suprime-Cam (HSC) survey imaging, I measure the structural parameters of low-redshift galaxies and connect them to the properties of their host dark matter halos via weak gravitational lensing and abundance matching. This work sheds light on the physical processes that set galaxy size during and after formation.

Star Formation in Early-Type Galaxies

Despite being traditionally considered "red and dead," a subset of early-type galaxies shows signatures of residual or rejuvenated star formation. I investigate the origin and drivers of this activity using multi-wavelength photometry and spectroscopic diagnostics, focusing on the role of environment and minor mergers.

Galaxy Morphology and Visual Structure

Galaxy structure encodes the history of assembly. I apply non-parametric morphological diagnostics to large photometric surveys to quantify how galaxy appearance correlates with stellar mass, environment, and star-formation history at low redshift.
